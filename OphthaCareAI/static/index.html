Quick Clinical Reference

Glaucoma Red Flags

  • Severe eye pain + red eye + haloes + blurred vision: acute angle closure EMERGENCY
  • Often completely asymptomatic until significant damage — regular screening essential
  • Family history: first-degree relatives should have regular eye exams from age 40
  • IOP measurement alone does NOT diagnose or exclude glaucoma

Retinal Emergency Signs

  • Sudden shower of floaters + flashes: same-day assessment (retinal tear/detachment)
  • Curtain/shadow across part of visual field: retinal detachment until proven otherwise
  • Sudden painless loss of central vision: wet AMD or vascular occlusion
  • Sudden profound painless vision loss: retinal artery occlusion — EMERGENCY

Chemical Eye Injury First Aid

  • Irrigate IMMEDIATELY with any available clean water for 20-30 minutes
  • Do NOT stop to seek help first — irrigate before anything else
  • Alkali burns (cement, bleach) cause deeper damage than acid burns
  • Urgent ophthalmology after irrigation

Giant Cell Arteritis (over 50s)

  • New headache + scalp tenderness + jaw pain + any visual symptoms
  • IMMEDIATE high-dose corticosteroids — do not wait for biopsy
  • Can cause sudden irreversible bilateral blindness if untreated
  • Urgent ESR/CRP + same-day ophthalmology referral
The Silent Thief of Sight

Glaucoma

Research on glaucoma diagnosis, monitoring, and management from AAO, RCOphth and NICE guidelines.

🚨
ACUTE ANGLE-CLOSURE GLAUCOMA: Severe eye pain, redness, blurred vision, haloes, nausea, fixed mid-dilated pupil — EYE CASUALTY IMMEDIATELY. Time-critical to prevent permanent vision loss.

Glaucoma Overview

Group of optic neuropathies causing progressive irreversible visual field loss. Second leading cause of blindness globally. Often asymptomatic in the early stages — earning the name the silent thief of sight. Requires lifelong management and monitoring.

Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

Most common type. Aqueous humour outflow impaired at the trabecular meshwork despite an anatomically open drainage angle. Risk factors: raised IOP, age over 40, family history (6x risk with first-degree relative), African ancestry, high myopia, thin central corneal thickness, diabetes.

Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Drainage angle physically blocked causing sudden dramatic IOP rise. EMERGENCY: severe eye pain, red eye, blurred vision, haloes around lights, headache, nausea/vomiting, fixed mid-dilated pupil, hazy/oedematous cornea. Predisposed: hyperopes, East Asian populations with anatomically narrow angles. Urgent IOP-lowering treatment then definitive laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI) to both eyes.

Normal Tension Glaucoma

Glaucomatous optic nerve damage and visual field loss despite IOP consistently within the statistically normal range. Vascular and other mechanisms may contribute in addition to any IOP-related damage. Managed similarly to POAG with IOP-lowering treatment targeting a lower pressure range.

Glaucoma Investigations

  • Goldmann Applanation Tonometry (IOP): Gold-standard IOP measurement. Normal approximately 10-21 mmHg but IOP alone does not diagnose or exclude glaucoma. Corneal thickness affects measured IOP (thin corneas underestimate true IOP).
  • Optic Disc Assessment: Slit-lamp biomicroscopy or indirect ophthalmoscopy. Cup-to-disc ratio over 0.6, asymmetry between eyes, neuroretinal rim notching, disc haemorrhages, and bayoneting of vessels are suspicious. Stereophotography for serial documentation and comparison.
  • Visual Field Testing (Humphrey 24-2 Perimetry): Detects and monitors functional loss. Characteristic patterns: arcuate scotoma, nasal step, altitudinal defect. Multiple fields needed to confirm progression due to test variability.
  • OCT RNFL (Retinal Nerve Fibre Layer): High-resolution structural imaging detects RNFL thinning, often before visual field changes. Serial analysis shows progression rate. Ganglion cell complex (GCC) analysis also useful.
  • Gonioscopy: Contact lens examination of the drainage angle — essential to classify as open or closed before initiating treatment.
  • Pachymetry: Central corneal thickness measurement for IOP correction and as independent progression risk factor.

Glaucoma Treatment

First-Line Eye Drops

  • Prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost 0.005% OD, travoprost, bimatoprost, tafluprost): Instilled once nightly. Most effective class — reduces IOP 25-35% by increasing uveoscleral outflow. Side effects: iris darkening (irreversible), eyelash growth, periorbital fat atrophy, conjunctival hyperaemia. Avoid in pregnancy.
  • Beta-blockers (timolol 0.25/0.5% BD or OD long-acting): Reduce aqueous production. CONTRAINDICATED: asthma, COPD, bradycardia, heart block, decompensated heart failure. Use nasolacrimal occlusion to reduce systemic absorption.
  • Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide BD/TDS, brinzolamide BD): Adjunct, reduce aqueous production. Oral acetazolamide for acute IOP spikes.
  • Alpha-2 agonists (brimonidine BD): Reduce production and increase uveoscleral outflow. Adjunct. Risk of ocular allergy over time.

Laser Treatment

  • Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT): First-line option, non-thermal laser to trabecular meshwork. Comparable to drops in the LiGHT trial. Approximately 70-80% respond. Repeatable.
  • Laser Peripheral Iridotomy (LPI): Creates hole in peripheral iris to open drainage angle. Definitive treatment for acute angle closure, prophylaxis for fellow eye and narrow-angle patients.

Surgery

  • Trabeculectomy: Most commonly performed filtration surgery. Creates new drainage pathway under conjunctival bleb. Long-term bleb-related infection risk.
  • MIGS (Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery): iStent, Hydrus, PRESERFLO, ab-interno canaloplasty. Safer profile, often combined with cataract surgery.
  • Tube shunts (Baerveldt, Ahmed): For refractory/failed trabeculectomy cases.

Glaucoma Monitoring

Glaucoma requires lifelong monitoring as it is a chronic progressive condition. Even well-controlled disease can progress and treatment may need escalating.

  • IOP measurement at every clinic visit
  • Optic disc photography for serial comparison
  • Visual fields: typically every 6-12 months in stable disease, more frequently if progressing
  • OCT RNFL: at least annually, more often if progression suspected
  • Adherence to drops reviewed at every visit — the most common cause of inadequate IOP control
  • Nasolacrimal occlusion technique (gentle finger pressure on inner corner of eye for 1-2 minutes after instilling drops) reduces systemic absorption significantly
  • Space different drops 5 minutes apart to prevent washout of the first drop
Medical Retina

Retinal Disease

Research on AMD, diabetic retinopathy, retinal detachment and anti-VEGF therapy from AAO and RCOphth guidelines.

Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

Leading cause of central vision loss in adults over 50 in developed countries. Affects the macula — responsible for detailed central vision used for reading, recognising faces, and driving.

Dry AMD (~90% of cases)

Drusen (lipid deposits) accumulate beneath the retinal pigment epithelium, causing gradual geographic atrophy and progressive painless central vision loss over years. No curative treatment currently. AREDS2 vitamin formulation (vitamin C, E, lutein, zeaxanthin, zinc) reduces risk of progression to advanced AMD in intermediate AMD. Regular Amsler grid self-monitoring recommended.

Wet AMD (~10% but accounts for most severe vision loss)

Abnormal choroidal neovascularisation leaks fluid and blood, causing rapid central vision distortion (metamorphopsia — straight lines appear wavy) or sudden central vision loss. URGENT referral — time to anti-VEGF treatment strongly affects outcome.

Anti-VEGF Treatment: Intravitreal injections of ranibizumab (Lucentis), aflibercept (Eylea), faricimab (Vabysmo), or bevacizumab (Avastin, off-label). Injected under topical anaesthesia in clinic. Monthly loading then treat-and-extend. Can stabilise or improve vision if started promptly. Newer agents (Eylea 8mg, Vabysmo) offer extended intervals up to 16 weeks for eligible patients.

Diabetic Retinopathy

Leading cause of blindness in working-age adults globally. Caused by chronic hyperglycaemia damaging retinal blood vessels. Annual screening is essential even when vision feels normal — early stages are asymptomatic.

Grading

  • Non-Proliferative DR (NPDR) mild/moderate/severe: Microaneurysms, haemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton wool spots
  • Proliferative DR (PDR): New vessel growth from disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE) due to retinal ischaemia — risk of vitreous haemorrhage and tractional detachment
  • Diabetic Macular Oedema (DMO): Central vision loss from macular fluid — most common cause of visual loss from DR, can occur at any DR stage

Management

  • Tight HbA1c and blood pressure control: most important interventions for prevention and slowing progression
  • Annual fundus photography screening: all diabetic patients as minimum
  • Anti-VEGF injections: first-line for DMO; also PDR (VIEW/PROTOCOL S trials)
  • Pan-retinal laser photocoagulation (PRP): for proliferative DR
  • Vitrectomy: for non-clearing vitreous haemorrhage or tractional retinal detachment

Retinal Detachment

🚨
New flashes + shower of floaters + curtain/shadow across vision = RETINAL DETACHMENT until proven otherwise. SAME-DAY ophthalmology assessment. Do NOT wait until morning.

Separation of the neurosensory retina from the underlying retinal pigment epithelium. Rhegmatogenous most common — a retinal tear allows liquid vitreous to track beneath the retina.

Risk Factors

  • High myopia (significantly increased lifetime risk)
  • Previous cataract surgery (posterior vitreous detachment more common)
  • Trauma to the eye
  • Family history of retinal detachment
  • Lattice retinal degeneration (peripheral retinal thinning)

Warning Symptoms

  • New sudden photopsia (flashes of light) — vitreous traction on the retina
  • Sudden shower of new floaters (cloud, rain, or cobwebs) — vitreous haemorrhage or pigment from a tear
  • Dark curtain, veil, or shadow progressing across the visual field — the detachment itself
  • Central vision loss if the macula is involved (macula-off)

Surgical Options

  • Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV): Remove vitreous, treat tears with laser/cryotherapy, internal tamponade with gas or silicone oil
  • Scleral buckle: External silicone band indents sclera to close retinal break
  • Pneumatic retinopexy: Intravitreal gas bubble + laser/cryo for selected superior tears

Other Retinal Conditions

Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)

Central (CRVO) or branch (BRVO) occlusion. Sudden painless blurred vision. Associated with hypertension, diabetes, glaucoma. Anti-VEGF injections first-line for macular oedema. Treat vascular risk factors. Ischaemic CRVO: monitor closely for neovascular glaucoma.

Retinal Artery Occlusion (RAO)

Sudden profound painless vision loss, typically embolic. OPHTHALMIC EMERGENCY — same-day assessment and urgent stroke/TIA pathway workup (carotid imaging, cardiac evaluation). Cherry-red spot at macula in CRAO.

Epiretinal Membrane (ERM) / Macular Pucker

Fibrous tissue over the macula surface causing central distortion (metamorphopsia) and/or blurred vision. Often stable and monitored. If significantly affecting vision, vitrectomy with membrane peel is effective.

Macular Hole

Full-thickness foveal defect. Central distortion and scotoma. Graded by OCT minimum diameter. Most benefit from vitrectomy + ILM peel + gas tamponade (post-operative face-down positioning required).

Ocular Pharmacology

Eye Drops & Medicines

Research on ophthalmic medications from AAO, RCOphth and NICE. Never change eye drops without your ophthalmologist's guidance.

⚠️
Eye drops have systemic effects via nasolacrimal absorption. Always use nasolacrimal occlusion (finger pressure on inner corner of eye for 1-2 minutes after instilling drops) to reduce systemic absorption.

Glaucoma Eye Drops

  • Prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost 0.005% OD, travoprost 0.004%, bimatoprost 0.01%, tafluprost 0.0015%): First-line. Once nightly. IOP reduction 25-35%. Side effects: iris colour change (irreversible), eyelash growth, periorbital fat atrophy. Not in pregnancy.
  • Beta-blockers (timolol 0.25/0.5% BD, long-acting OD): Reduce aqueous production. CONTRAINDICATED in asthma, COPD, bradycardia, heart block. Nasolacrimal occlusion essential to reduce systemic absorption.
  • Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide 2% TDS, brinzolamide 1% BD): Adjuncts, reduce aqueous. Stinging on instillation. Oral acetazolamide for acute spikes.
  • Alpha-2 agonists (brimonidine 0.1-0.2% BD): Adjunct. Risk of ocular allergy over time. Avoid in children under 2 years (CNS depression).
  • Fixed combinations (Cosopt = dorzolamide+timolol, Combigan = brimonidine+timolol, Xalacom = latanoprost+timolol): Improve compliance. Leave 5 minutes between separate drops.

Anti-VEGF and Intravitreal Agents

Intravitreal injections performed in clinic under topical anaesthesia with antiseptic preparation (povidone-iodine). Well-tolerated and effective for delivering drug directly to target tissue.

  • Ranibizumab (Lucentis): Anti-VEGF antibody fragment. Licensed wet AMD, DMO, RVO. NICE approved. Monthly then treat-and-extend or PRN.
  • Aflibercept (Eylea 2mg): Blocks VEGF-A, VEGF-B, PIGF. Licensed wet AMD, DMO, RVO. 8-weekly after loading. Eylea 8mg now available for 12-16 weekly intervals in eligible patients.
  • Faricimab (Vabysmo): Bispecific anti-VEGF-A and anti-Ang-2. Wet AMD and DMO. Up to 16-weekly intervals for eligible patients. Newest approved agent.
  • Bevacizumab (Avastin): Off-label full-length anti-VEGF. Comparable efficacy to ranibizumab (CATT trial), substantially lower cost. Widely used globally.
  • Dexamethasone implant (Ozurdex): Biodegradable steroid for macular oedema from RVO and non-infectious posterior uveitis. Duration 3-6 months. Risk of raised IOP and cataract with repeat use.

Anti-Infective Eye Drops

  • Chloramphenicol 0.5% drops / 1% ointment: First-line for bacterial conjunctivitis. Every 2 hours initially then QDS for 5 days. Ointment at night or for children.
  • Fusidic acid 1% gel (Fucithalmic): Alternative for bacterial conjunctivitis. BD dosing. Good Staph aureus coverage.
  • Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin 0.3%, moxifloxacin 0.5%, ofloxacin 0.3%): Bacterial keratitis (intensive dosing every 30-60 minutes initially), post-operative prophylaxis.
  • Aciclovir 3% ointment / ganciclovir 0.15% gel: Herpes simplex keratitis. 5x daily for 14 days. NEVER use topical steroids for HSV keratitis without specialist guidance.
  • PHMB 0.02% + propamidine (Brolene) 0.1%: Acanthamoeba keratitis. Intensive drops under specialist supervision. Extremely painful condition, high risk without aggressive treatment.

Steroids and Anti-Inflammatory Eye Drops

  • Prednisolone acetate 1% (Pred Forte): Main steroid for anterior uveitis — hourly initially in severe cases, very gradual taper based on response. Also post-op cataract surgery.
  • Dexamethasone 0.1%, betamethasone 0.1%, fluorometholone 0.1%: Fluorometholone softer for longer use. All carry IOP-raising risk (steroid responders ~30%, check IOP if on drops for 2+ weeks).
  • STEROID RED FLAG: NEVER use topical steroids for a red eye without ophthalmologist guidance. Can dramatically worsen herpes simplex keratitis, fungal keratitis, and bacterial keratitis.
  • NSAIDs (ketorolac, diclofenac, bromfenac, nepafenac): Post-operative, prophylaxis/treatment of cystoid macular oedema after cataract surgery, PRK pain management.
  • Ciclosporin 0.05/0.1% (Restasis, Ikervis): Topical immunosuppressant for moderate-severe dry eye with inflammatory component. Takes 3-6 months for full effect.

Lubricants, Dilating Drops and Other Agents

  • Artificial tears (hyaluronic acid, carmellose, carbomer, HPMC): First-line for dry eye. Preservative-free formulations strongly preferred for regular use (more than 4x/day) and contact lens wearers to avoid preservative toxicity. Gels/ointments for severe dry eye and night-time use.
  • Cycloplegics & mydriatics: Cyclopentolate 1% (2-3h, cycloplegic refraction in children, anterior uveitis), atropine 1% (7-14 days, children, myopia control), tropicamide 0.5/1% (4-6h, routine pupil dilation), phenylephrine 2.5/10% (dilation adjunct, no cycloplegia).
  • Antihistamine/mast cell stabilisers (olopatadine, ketotifen, nedocromil, sodium cromoglicate): Allergic conjunctivitis. Start before peak season for seasonal allergy. Olopatadine preferred for year-round disease.
  • Pilocarpine 1-4%: Cholinergic miotic for angle closure, historically for POAG. Side effects: induced myopia, brow ache. Largely superseded for routine glaucoma management.
Ophthalmic Surgery

Eye Surgery

Research on common ophthalmic surgical procedures from AAO, RCOphth and NICE guidelines.

Cataract Surgery (Phacoemulsification)

The most commonly performed surgical procedure in the world. Day case, local anaesthetic (topical drops or injection). Small incision (2.2-2.8mm), capsulorhexis, ultrasound phacoemulsification of the cataractous lens, aspiration, IOL insertion into the capsular bag.

  • Monofocal IOL: Fixed single focal point (typically distance). Clear predictable optics, glasses needed for near/intermediate. Most common IOL type worldwide.
  • Multifocal / EDOF IOL: Multiple or extended focal zones to reduce spectacle dependence. May cause dysphotopsia (haloes, starbursts). Less suitable if significant macular disease present.
  • Toric IOL: Corrects pre-existing corneal astigmatism. Requires accurate biometry and careful IOL orientation. Improves unaided distance vision.
  • PCO (Posterior Capsule Opacification): Most common complication, up to 30% within 5 years. Quick, painless outpatient YAG laser capsulotomy restores vision within minutes.

Refractive Surgery

  • LASIK: Femtosecond or microkeratome flap creation, excimer laser reshapes corneal stroma, flap repositioned. Rapid recovery. Myopia up to -10D, hyperopia up to +4D. Not suitable for thin corneas, keratoconus, or irregular astigmatism.
  • LASEK / PRK (Surface Ablation): No flap. Epithelium removed, laser applied to corneal surface. Slower recovery (1-2 weeks), more post-op discomfort. Better for thin corneas and contact sports. Long-term results equivalent to LASIK.
  • SMILE (Small Incision Lenticule Extraction): Femtosecond laser creates intrastromal lenticule extracted through a small incision. Flapless. For myopia and astigmatism.
  • Phakic IOL (Visian ICL, EVO): Additional lens implanted in front of natural lens for high refractive errors outside laser range. Reversible. Excellent optical quality.

Vitreoretinal and Other Surgery

  • Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV): Small-gauge instruments (23g, 25g, 27g) for retinal detachment, vitreous haemorrhage, ERM/macular hole peel, endophthalmitis, dropped nuclear fragment.
  • Trabeculectomy: Filtration surgery creating new aqueous drainage under conjunctival bleb. Most commonly performed glaucoma filtration surgery.
  • Corneal Transplant (PK, DSAEK, DMEK): Full-thickness (PK) for scarring/advanced keratoconus; selective endothelial replacement (DSAEK/DMEK) for Fuchs' dystrophy. Better outcomes and lower rejection rates with lamellar techniques.
  • Strabismus Surgery: Recession (weakening) or resection (strengthening) of extraocular muscles to correct misalignment. May need multiple procedures.

Understanding Your Prescription

A spectacle/contact lens prescription: Sphere (SPH) — degree of myopia (minus) or hyperopia (plus). Cylinder (CYL) — astigmatism power. Axis — direction of astigmatism (1-180 degrees). Addition (ADD) — extra near power for presbyopia. Prism — for double vision correction.

Example: -3.50 / -1.25 x 175 ADD +2.00 = 3.5D myopia with 1.25D astigmatism at axis 175 degrees, plus +2.00D reading addition (presbyopia).

Eye Emergency Guide

🚨
EYE EMERGENCIES — Eye Casualty / 112 (India) / 999 (UK) / 911 (US): Chemical splash (IRRIGATE FIRST 20+ min), penetrating injury (rigid shield, NO pressure), sudden vision loss, acute angle closure (severe pain + haloes + red eye), new flashes/floaters with curtain, GCA with visual symptoms in over-50s.

🚨 Chemical Eye Injury

IRRIGATE IMMEDIATELY — do not stop to seek help first. Use any available clean water.

  • Hold eye open under running tap or pour water over the eye continuously
  • Irrigate for a MINIMUM of 20-30 minutes
  • Check pH with litmus paper if available — continue until neutral (pH 7)
  • Alkali burns (cement, plaster, bleach, ammonia, oven cleaner) cause deeper penetration than acid burns
  • Remove any solid particles gently if visible
  • Attend eye casualty AFTER irrigation for grading and ongoing management

🚨 Penetrating Eye Injury

  • DO NOT apply any pressure to the eye
  • DO NOT attempt to remove any embedded foreign body
  • DO NOT instil any drops or ointment
  • Place a rigid shield (NOT an eye pad) — a clean cup or cardboard dome for protection
  • Keep patient calm and still; nil by mouth if surgery may be needed
  • Attend eye casualty immediately — risk of endophthalmitis and vision loss

🚨 Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Recognition: Severe eye pain, red eye, blurred vision, haloes around lights, headache, nausea/vomiting. Fixed mid-dilated pupil, hazy/oedematous cornea. Very high IOP (often 40-70 mmHg).

  • EYE CASUALTY IMMEDIATELY — rapid IOP rise causes irreversible optic nerve damage within hours
  • Do NOT give dilating drops (worsens angle closure)
  • IOP-lowering treatment: topical drops, IV/oral acetazolamide, IV mannitol in severe cases
  • Definitive: laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI) to affected eye and prophylactic LPI to fellow eye
  • Can mimic migraine or acute abdomen — always check IOP in any patient with nausea/vomiting and eye pain

🚨 Sudden Vision Loss

  • Any sudden unexplained vision loss: SAME-DAY ophthalmology assessment
  • Flashes + shower of floaters + curtain/shadow: retinal detachment — same-day
  • Sudden central distortion (wavy lines): wet AMD — urgent (days)
  • Profound sudden painless vision loss: retinal artery occlusion — emergency (cardiovascular workup)
  • Giant cell arteritis in over-50s with headache: EMERGENCY — immediate steroids to protect the other eye
  • Red tinge/floaters in diabetic patient: vitreous haemorrhage — urgent

Other Eye Emergencies

  • Giant Cell Arteritis (over-50s) — MEDICAL EMERGENCY: New headache + scalp tenderness/jaw pain + any visual symptoms. Urgent ESR/CRP + IMMEDIATE high-dose prednisolone. Do NOT wait for biopsy. Can cause sudden irreversible bilateral blindness.
  • Endophthalmitis (post-operative): Increasing pain, redness, and decreasing vision days after eye surgery — same-day emergency. Urgent intravitreal antibiotics required.
  • Hyphema (blood in anterior chamber): After blunt trauma. Restricted activity, avoid aspirin/NSAIDs (increased rebleed risk). Ophthalmology review urgently.
  • Orbital cellulitis: Periorbital swelling + proptosis + restricted/painful eye movements + fever — urgent CT and IV antibiotics. Distinguish from preseptal cellulitis (no proptosis or restricted movements).
  • Third nerve palsy with dilated pupil: Ptosis + eye in down-and-out position + dilated unreactive pupil — NEUROSURGICAL EMERGENCY (posterior communicating artery aneurysm until excluded). Urgent CT/MR angiography.
Contact Lens Safety

Contact Lens Safety & Care

Evidence-based contact lens safety guidance to protect your vision.

🚨
Contact lens pain, redness, significant light sensitivity, or blurred vision: REMOVE LENSES IMMEDIATELY and seek same-day assessment. Microbial keratitis can progress rapidly and threaten vision.

Essential Safety Rules

  • Always wash and thoroughly dry hands before handling contact lenses — the single most important hygiene step.
  • NEVER use tap water to rinse, store, or prepare solutions for contact lenses. Tap water carries Acanthamoeba — a parasite causing severe, sight-threatening, extremely painful keratitis strongly associated with water exposure and contact lens wear.
  • Replace your lens case monthly. Never top up old solution — always use fresh solution. Air-dry the case face-down on a clean tissue between uses.
  • Wear only for the prescribed schedule: daily disposables = discard each evening; fortnightly/monthly = only up to the intended replacement date. Extending wear dramatically increases infection risk.
  • Do not sleep in lenses unless specifically prescribed for extended/overnight wear.
  • Do not swim or shower in reusable lenses. Daily disposables removed immediately afterwards are lower risk but still not zero risk.

Warning Signs — Remove and Seek Same-Day Assessment

  • Eye pain beyond mild initial sensation when inserting
  • Increasing redness not resolving after lens removal
  • Significant light sensitivity (photophobia)
  • Blurred vision not clearing with blinking
  • Excessive watering or discharge
  • Sensation of something stuck in the eye
